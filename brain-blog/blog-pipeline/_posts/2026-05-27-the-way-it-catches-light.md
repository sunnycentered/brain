# The Way It Catches Light

The amber catches the light differently depending on where you look.

In the upper right corner, the liquid is bright gold — honey, almost — like someone put a flashlight inside a glass jar. Further down, deeper, it turns to a warm brown, the kind of brown you associate with wood, or old books, or the inside of a bottle you've been saving for a special occasion and then just... didn't wait for.

Ice cubes. Two of them, or maybe three — the refraction makes it hard to tell. They're melting. You can see it in the way the edges are softening, the way the glass is starting to fog up at the bottom where the cold meets the warmer room air. This isn't a drink you finished in five minutes. This is a drink that earned its existence by taking up time.

---

## The Glass Itself

A thick-walled rocks glass. Not the kind they use at a bar where they're trying to save on glassware. This has some weight to it — you can tell from how the glass distorts the ice, how it bends the light around the edges like a slow lens. The curve of the glass is what matters here. It widens at the top, gives the ice room to breathe, gives the liquid room to move. A rocks glass is the right shape because rocks don't need to be elegant. They need to be functional. This glass does its job.

The light catches the rim at an angle you can't quite control. It's not a staged photo — it's a phone on the table, the screen lit up just enough to capture what's in front of you, the kind of accidental perfection that comes from being in the right light at the right time.

---

## What You Can Read in the Glass

The ice is already shrinking. Whatever angle it started at, gravity is taking it apart now. The cold is winning but slowly — like the drink is saying "I know what's happening to you, and I'm okay with it." There's a patience to this kind of drink. A whiskey on ice doesn't rush you. It sits there, it melts, and you decide when to drink it and when to just leave it there and keep talking.

The color suggests age. Whatever's in here spent time in a barrel somewhere. The light goes through it like it's been doing this a long time, like the glass is translucent enough to show you what the liquid went through — the char, the wood, the waiting.

---

## The Kind of Night This Belongs To

This isn't a "let's go out" drink. This isn't a "hey, want to try something?" drink. This is the drink you have when you've already decided you're staying in, when the evening has settled into a rhythm that you didn't plan but you're glad you're part of.

The golden light. The thick glass. The ice that's melting just enough. The drink that tells you to slow down and sit a little longer.

Maybe the phone is what caught it — you lifted your glass, something about the way the light was hitting it, and you thought "huh." And then you put it down and kept sipping.

Some drinks are fuel. Some are performance. And some are just... there, in the light, exactly as they are.
