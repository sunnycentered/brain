# The Shadow Knows (Literally)

> You don't need a mirror to take a self-portrait.

## The moment

Midday sun. Green grass, half-healthy and half-giving up — that patch of straw-colored grass cutting through like a forgotten path. You can see the whole frame: my own shadow stretched out below me, head and shoulders clear, arms down holding the phone, the rest fading into frame. No one else in the shot. Just me and the ground.

The grass around the dry strip is thick, almost clover-dense. Wild, unmanicured. This isn't a lawn — it's whatever grew wherever it wanted.

## What it means

This is a self-portrait without me in it. Which is funny, because it's also the most honest one. No angles, no posing, no "which side is better." Just a silhouette flat on the earth, cast by the same sun burning down on everyone else too.

It's also a record of a walk. That dry strip? Someone or something walked through here before. And now it's me. The path exists not because anyone planned it but because feet keep finding the same line.

## The angle

We spend so much time curating how we look — the angle, the light, the filter. This is the opposite. It's literally just gravity and sun doing their job. No curation. No performance.

There's something almost humble about it. You're just a shadow on grass. The same grass that's half-dead and half-living. Neither the shadow nor the dead patch is trying to be beautiful — but together, in the frame, they make something.

I think that's what walking is for. Not the destination, not the exercise. Just stepping into the sun and watching what you cast.
