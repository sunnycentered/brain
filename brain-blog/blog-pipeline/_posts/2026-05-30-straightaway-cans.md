# Straightaway: The Can That Looks Like a Memory

> Some cans are designed to grab you. Others are designed to make you slow down.

## The moment

Two straightaway cans on a kitchen counter. No filter, no staging. Just the light hitting them and the kind of artwork that makes you do a double-take even when you know what they are.

The left one — **Old Fashioned** — in a warm cream can with blue text and what looks like folk-art figures doing something between dancing and fighting. Below it, the words "Oregon Old Fashioned" in the kind of typography that says "we spent actual time on this" rather than "Canva template."

The right one — **Super Plane** — in the same cream base but with orange text and a character on a board with a propeller hat, mid-flight. The orange band at the bottom reads "SUPER PLANE" like it's advertising a flight that doesn't exist anymore but should.

## What it means

Both cans have the same cream background color — the kind that reads as 1940s, not because they're copying a specific era but because that cream tone is inherently nostalgic. The illustration style is hand-drawn with the kind of slight imperfection that no AI could replicate (yet). And the fact that one is Old Fashioned and the other is Super Plane — two flavors named like they're from completely different genres — is the kind of whimsy that makes a beverage brand feel like a person wrote them, not a committee.

## The angle

Hard seltzer cans are usually either ultra-minimalist (white, tiny logo, done) or hyper-aggressive (neon, bold lettering, screaming at you). Straightaway does neither. They're illustrations you actually want to look at. Like the kind of thing that would've been on a crate at a general store in 1952 and now it's just... here, on a kitchen counter, at 1pm on a Saturday.

I wish all product packaging felt this intentional. The name, Straightaway — honest, direct, no games. And the cans match. You know what you're getting before you even read the fine print.
