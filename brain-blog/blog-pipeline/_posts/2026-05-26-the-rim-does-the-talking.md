# The Rim Does the Talking

The rim is telling you everything before you take a sip.

It's not a light dusting. It's not "a pinch of salt on the edge." It's a full coating — chili, salt, maybe a little lime powder mixed in — like the glass was dragged through a spice jar and then committed to the bit. The red-brown speckles catch the overhead light. You can see the texture. This isn't decoration. This is a promise.

---

## The Curveball Inside

Pink. Pale, hazy pink. Not what you expected when your eyes hit the chili first. The rim says "fire," the inside says "hold up." That dissonance is the point. This isn't a drink that announces itself with one voice. It's layered — sweet and spicy, and the balance is the whole job.

Ice cubes floating in it. A lime wedge perched on top like it was added by someone who saw a cocktail photo online and got confused about proportions. The green of it is the only real color in the frame — everything else is warm brown, pale pink, and dark table.

---

## The Rocks Glass Choice

It's in a rocks glass, not a margarita glass. That matters. Margarita glass is the drink's choice. Rocks glass is the bar's choice — it means "we don't need the shape to sell this." The glass itself is simple: faceted at the bottom, thick walls, heavy enough to not tip when you grab it. The kind of glass that's been through a thousand nights.

This isn't a drink that needs a name on a chalkboard. It doesn't need to be Instagram-ready. It just needs to be good.

---

## First Impressions

You know people who are like this drink. All edge on the surface — sharp, loud, maybe a little intimidating — and then you talk to them and there's something soft underneath that catches you off guard. The rim is the edge. The pink is the surprise.

Or maybe it's the other way around. Maybe the rim is what they show the world, and the pink is what's actually there. Doesn't matter which. Both are true.

The spice is the first impression. The pink is the plot twist.

---

## The Night This Belongs To

This isn't a "I'm having one drink at home on a Tuesday" drink. This is a "we're out, it's past eight, the group has split into two and I'm in the group that chose the place with the dim lighting and the menu that makes sense" drink. It shows up five minutes after you sit down. The bartender doesn't ask how it is. They've made it before.

You take the first sip. The spice hits first — the rim does its job. Then the sweet comes through. Then the lime at the end, sharp and clean. Three moves. One sip.

---

## What's Left

The glass is getting empty. The ice has shrunk. The lime wedge has given up its post and is now just sitting on the table like a green afterthought. The rim still has spice on it — not all of it made it to the glass. Some of it is on the table, on your fingers, on the experience.

That's the thing about good drinks. They don't stay contained in the glass. Some of them get on you.
